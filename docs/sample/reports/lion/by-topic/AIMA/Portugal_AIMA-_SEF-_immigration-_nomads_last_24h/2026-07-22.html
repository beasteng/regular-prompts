The following summary outlines the most recent developments in Portugal regarding immigration, the AIMA agency, and related policies as of July 22, 2026.

  • AIMA Backlog Reduction: A dedicated government task force has successfully cleared nearly 20% of the total pending AIMA case backlog within the last three months, signaling a gradual improvement in service capacity.
  • Automatic Social Security Numbers: Starting in late July, AIMA will implement a new system to automatically assign Social Security numbers (NISS) during the regularization process, eliminating the need for immigrants to visit separate agencies.
  • Stricter Regularization Rules: The Portuguese Parliament has approved measures to end previous "expression of interest" routes, specifically closing pathways for regularization via professional training or through children of foreign nationals studying in Portugal for those who entered as tourists.
  • Online Process Tracking: Applicants can now monitor the status of their residence permits, renewals, and card production online through the official AIMA contact form at contactenos.aima.gov.pt.
  • Temporary Protection for Ukrainians: Portugal continues to uphold its temporary protection regime for those fleeing the war in Ukraine, with the status now aligned with the European Council's extension until March 4, 2027.
  • Extension of Residency Validity: Authorities have confirmed that residence permits remain subject to ongoing renewal procedures, with specific online portals available for those expiring in July.
  • Digital Nomad (D8) Visa Requirements: As of mid-2026, the D8 Digital Nomad Visa requires a minimum monthly income of €3,680 for the main applicant, with specific percentage increases for accompanying family members.
  • Impact on Labor Market: The government's recent tightening of immigration policies is facing criticism from industry leaders in sectors like construction, who report that these changes are worsening labor shortages and hindering housing crisis solutions.
  • Continued Operation of AIMA: AIMA remains the primary authority for administrative immigration matters following the closure of the former SEF, with continued efforts to stabilize its operations despite past staffing and backlog challenges.
  • Golden Visa Remains Active: Despite broader reforms to nationality and immigration laws, the Golden Visa program remains functional and continues to be used by international families for long-term residency and investment planning.
