Here is a summary of the most important news from Portugal regarding changes to the Agency for Integration, Migration and Asylum (AIMA), the remnants of the transition from the Foreigners and Borders Service (SEF), temporary protection for Ukrainians, and digital nomad visas.

As immigration policies change rapidly, this list features breaking updates from the last 24 hours, alongside key ongoing structural developments:

  • Judicial Task Force Clears 18% of Backlogged AIMA Lawsuits: A special judicial task force consisting of 28 administrative judges has processed 22,436 immigrant lawsuits filed against AIMA over the last three months. This successfully resolves nearly one-fifth of the 124,000 lawsuit backlog generated by severe delays in initial residency processing. (Source: The Portugal News)
  • Migration Workers Union Meets with President Over Hate Speech and Consular Shortages: In a high-level meeting at the Belém Palace, the Union of Migration Workers (STM) expressed deep concern over rising anti-immigrant hate speech in Portugal. The union requested immediate reinforcements for AIMA staff working in the understaffed Portuguese consular network to encourage safer, legal migration pathways. (Source: The Portugal News)
  • Strict Migration Curbs Heavily Impacting Portugal's Construction Sector: Industry executives have warned that the government's rapid tightening of immigration policies—specifically the elimination of the "Manifestation of Interest" mechanism—is choking the country's labor supply. The resulting shortage of workers is actively delaying efforts to build affordable housing and address the nation's severe real estate crisis. (Source: Reuters)
  • Automatic NISS Assignment Set to Launch at AIMA Appointments: A new digital integration between AIMA and Social Security will launch in late July. When immigrants attend their regularisation appointments, they will automatically be assigned their Social Security Identification Number (NISS) in real-time. This eliminates the need to visit separate social security counters, saving roughly 250,000 visits per year. (Source: GrowIN Portugal)
  • AIMA Accused of Accumulating Debts to Legal Professionals: The Livre political party has formally questioned the government following reports that AIMA owes over €88,000 in outstanding payments to lawyers and solicitors. These professionals were contracted under a cooperation agreement to help speed up the processing of pending residency applications. (Source: Observador)
  • EU Extends Ukrainian Temporary Protection Until 2028 with Military Compliance Clause: EU member states, including Portugal, have agreed to extend temporary protection for displaced Ukrainians until March 4, 2028. However, under a newly introduced restriction, any new applicants must prove they have complied with their military obligations in Ukraine to qualify. (Source: Council of the EU)
  • Portugal's Automatic Alignment on Ukrainian Temporary Protection Limits: Portugal continues to align its domestic temporary protection policies with the Council of the EU's directives. The simplified regime automatically issues temporary residence permits, registers refugees across Portuguese tax, social security, and health services, and grants them full working rights. (Source: Ukraine is Home Portal)
  • New Online AIMA Portal Options for EU Permanent Residence: AIMA has expanded its "Portal das Renovações" to handle permanent residence applications for EU citizens and their families. It replaces previous manual contact forms, moving Article 16 (for EU nationals) and Article 17 (for non-EU family members) applications to a fully digital channel to cut backlogs. (Source: The Portugal News)
  • SEF to AIMA Post-Abolition Division Remains Fully Active: Years after the discussion of SEF's extinction began, the divide of powers is complete. Administrative functions are fully managed by AIMA, while the Polícia Judiciária (PJ) handles illegal immigration and human trafficking investigations. Sea and land border controls are operated strictly by the GNR, and airport security is run by the PSP. (Source: The End of the SEF Overview)
  • Portugal D8 Digital Nomad Visa Income Limits Raised to €3,680 per Month: To qualify for the D8 Digital Nomad Visa, applicants must now show active monthly remote work income of at least €3,680 (calculated as four times Portugal's minimum wage). Additionally, strict individual savings rules require proof of personal funds starting at €11,040. (Source: Portugal D8 Digital Nomad Guide)
